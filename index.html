Welcome!

In this data visualization, I explore the relationship between overall government expenditure and education and crime rates. While government spending is not specifically focused on these areas, I aim to provide insight into the potential impact of overall government spending on education and crime rates. The comprehensive data set includes information from the Sustainable Development Goal indicators compiled by the United Nations, academic surveys, and international financial institutions. This data visualization is a useful tool for policymakers, researchers, and anyone interested in understanding the potential impact of overall government spending on education and crime rates.

The Variables Explained

Crime index

The Crime Index is an estimation of the overall level of crime in a given country, based on surveys. The surveys use a range of values from -2 to +2, which are then filtered to eliminate potential spam, and presented on a scale of 0 to 100. The index is updated using data up to 36 months old and is remade twice per year. The Safety Index is the opposite of the Crime Index, with a high Safety Index indicating a very safe city. The actual formulas used to calculate the indices are subject to change, but at present, complex empirical formulas can be found here.

Country Expenses (% of GDP)

As defined in the source "Expense is cash payments for operating activities of the government in providing goods and services. It includes compensation of employees (such as wages and salaries), interest and subsidies, grants, social benefits, and other expenses such as rent and dividends." This information is taken from the World Development Indicators

"Educational attainment, at least Bachelor's or equivalent

To determine the indicator, the percentage of individuals aged 25 and older who have completed a Bachelor's degree or an equivalent qualification is calculated. This is achieved by dividing the number of people who meet this criterion by the total population of that age group, then multiplying the result by 100. If the resulting value is zero or so small that it would round to zero, the number 0 is used instead. The UNESCO Institute for Statistics is responsible for collecting the data, which is primarily sourced from national population censuses, household surveys, and labor force surveys.

Correlogram

This Correlogram is a graphical and numeric representation of the Pearson correlation. This coefficient is a measure of the linear correlation between the variables. It ranges from -1 to 1, where -1 indicates a perfect negative correlation, 0 indicates no correlation, and 1 indicates a perfect positive correlation.

2D Density

Contour lines represent areas with the same density or frequency of data points, forming a topographical representation of the data. The crime weight is used to determine the density or frequency of the points, with higher crime weights contributing to a higher density in the corresponding area on the graph.

When creating a contour density graph using D3, you can manipulate two important parameters: bandwidth and threshold. These parameters can be adjusted using sliders for a more interactive experience.

Bandwidth: This parameter controls the smoothness of the density estimation. A smaller bandwidth will result in a more detailed and "noisy" graph, with closely spaced contour lines, while a larger bandwidth will produce a smoother and more generalized representation of the data with wider spaced contour lines. Adjusting the bandwidth slider allows you to explore different levels of detail in the density estimation.

Threshold: This parameter determines the number of contour levels or lines to display in the graph. A lower threshold value will result in more contour lines, representing smaller density intervals, while a higher threshold value will reduce the number of contour lines, representing larger density intervals. Adjusting the threshold slider helps you focus on specific density ranges and filter out less significant areas.

Contour density graphs are particularly useful for understanding complex relationships and patterns in data. They can reveal trends, correlations, and outliers, as well as help identify areas of high or low concentration. In the case of the expense-education-crime graph, a contour density graph can show how crime rates vary across different combinations of expense and education levels, revealing insights that might not be immediately apparent in a table or scatterplot.

Conclusion

Upon close examination of the available data, it can be inferred that although there seems to be a slight correlation between government spending, education, and crime rates, it is not possible to establish a clear and unequivocal relationship among these variables. While it is tempting to draw conclusions based on the available evidence, it is important to note that other factors may be at play that are not captured by the current visualization.

There are several potential confounding variables that could be affecting the observed relationship between government spending, education, and crime rates. For example, socio-economic status, cultural factors, and demographic characteristics of the population could all have an impact on these variables. In addition, the quality and accessibility of social services, such as healthcare and housing, may also be significant factors that affect crime rates.

Therefore, it is critical to recognize the potential influence of these extraneous factors and undertake more extensive research to examine their impact on the relationship between government spending, education, and crime rates. By delving into these additional factors and accounting for their effects, it will be possible to gain a more complete understanding of the complex relationships between these variables and to develop more effective policies and strategies to address issues related to crime rates, education, and government spending.
